Chicago

On the shore of Lake Michigan, Chicago was incorporated as a city in 1837
near a portage between the Great Lakes and the Mississippi River watershed.
It grew rapidly in the mid-19th century; by 1860, Chicago was the youngest
U.S. city to exceed a population of 100,000. Even after 1871, when the Great
Chicago Fire destroyed several square miles and left more than 100,000 homeless
Chicago's population grew to 503,000 by 1880 and then doubled to more than a
million within the decade. The construction boom accelerated population
growth throughout the following decades, and by 1900, less than 30 years
after the fire, Chicago was the fifth-largest city in the world. Chicago
made noted contributions to urban planning and zoning standards, including
new construction styles (such as, Chicago School architecture, the
development of the City Beautiful Movement, and the steel-framed skyscraper).
